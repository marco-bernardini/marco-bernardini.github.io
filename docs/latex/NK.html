The New Keynesian Model: logic of the derivation

This note reconstructs the baseline 3-equations New Keynesian model in the style of Jordi Galì, based on lectures by Andrea Ferrero. The aim is not just to report formulas, but to explain why each block is introduced and what job it does.

The necessity for the model arises from the fact that in the classical RBC model money is neutral, and therefore monetary policy does not have any real effect. Clearly, in order to study the effect on such policy changes in the real economy, we need to construct a new model.
In doing so, we combine:
1. monopolistic competition, so firms set prices as a markup over marginal cost, and
2. nominal rigidity, so not all firms can reset prices every period.

Without the first, there is no meaningful pricing problem. Without the second, monetary policy remains neutral.

1. Retailers and Wholesalers

We introduce two layers of production.

  • Retailers are perfectly competitive and combine differentiated intermediate goods into one final good.
  • Wholesalers produce the differentiated intermediate goods and have monopoly power.

This split is not economically deep in itself. It is mainly a tractable way to generate a downward-sloping demand curve for each producer. One could equivalently put love for variety directly in household preferences, but the retailer layer is algebraically cleaner.

We will proceed as follow

  1. build a final-good aggregator,
  2. derive demand for each differentiated good,
  3. then let intermediate firms solve a price-setting problem.

2. Retailers

Retailers are competitive. They choose the bundle of differentiated goods \(Y_t(i)\) to produce a final good \(Y_t\):

\[ Y_t=\left[\int_0^1 Y_t(i)^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\,di\right]^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}}, \qquad \theta>1. \]

They solve

\[ \min_{\{Y_t(i)\}} \int_0^1 P_t(i)Y_t(i)\,di \quad \text{s.t.} \quad Y_t=\left[\int_0^1 Y_t(i)^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\,di\right]^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}}. \]

The economic role of this problem is simple: retailers do not set prices, they instead just choose the cheapest mix of varieties to produce one unit of the final good. The solution to their problem gives the demand curve faced by each wholesale firm.

Define the price index

\[ P_t=\left[\int_0^1 P_t(i)^{1-\theta}\,di\right]^{\frac{1}{1-\theta}}. \]

Then set the Lagrangian

\[ \mathcal{L} = \int_0^1 P_t(i) Y_t(i)\, di + \Lambda_t \left[ Y_t - \left( \int_0^1 Y_t(i)^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}} di \right)^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}} \right] \]

\[ P_t(i) = \Lambda_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}} \, Y_t(i)^{-\frac{1}{\theta}} \]

\[ P_t(i)^{1-\theta} = \left(\Lambda_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\right)^{1-\theta} \, Y_t(i)^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}} \]

\[ \int_0^1 P_t(i)^{1-\theta} \, di = \left(\Lambda_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\right)^{1-\theta} \int_0^1 Y_t(i)^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}} \, di \]

\[ \int_0^1 P_t(i)^{1-\theta} \, di = \left(\Lambda_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\right)^{1-\theta} \, Y_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}} \]

\[ P_t = \left(\Lambda_t^{\frac{\theta-1}{\theta}}\right) \, Y_t^{-\frac{1}{\theta}} \]

\[ P_t = P_t(i)\, Y_t(i)^{\frac{1}{\theta}} \, Y_t^{-\frac{1}{\theta}} \]

\[ Y_t(i) = Y_t \left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta} \]

\[ \frac{d \log Y_t(i)}{d \log P_t(i)} = -\theta \]

Then the standard CES demand system is

\[ Y_t(i)=Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

This is the first key object in the model.

Interpretation:

  • if firm \(i\) raises its price relative to the aggregate price level, demand for its good falls;
  • the elasticity of that fall is \(\theta\);
  • \(\theta\) therefore controls market power.

This is why the retailer problem exists at all.

3. Wholesalers: why markup pricing appears

Each wholesale firm \(i\) produces with labor only:

\[ Y_t(i)=A_tN_t(i). \]

So there is no capital here. That is deliberate: Ferrero wants the simplest environment in which monetary non-neutrality appears.

Each firm faces the demand curve derived above:

\[ Y_t(i)=Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

Profits in real terms are

\[ \max_{P_t(i),N_t(i)} (1-\tau_t)\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}Y_t(i)-\frac{W_t}{P_t}N_t(i) \]

subject to

\[ Y_t(i)=A_tN_t(i), \qquad Y_t(i)=Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

The tax/subsidy term \(\tau_t\) is useful because later it lets us offset monopoly distortions.

From production,

\[ N_t(i)=\frac{Y_t(i)}{A_t}. \]

Hence real marginal cost is

\[ MC_t=\frac{W_t}{A_tP_t}. \]

So profits can be rewritten as

\[ \max_{P_t(i)} \left[ (1-\tau_t)\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}-MC_t \right]Y_t(i) \]

with

\[ Y_t(i)=Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

Substituting demand:

\[ \max_{P_t(i)} \left[ (1-\tau_t)\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}-MC_t \right] Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

Differentiating with respect to \(P_t(i)\) gives

\[ \frac{P_t(i)}{P_t} = MC_t\frac{\theta}{(1-\tau_t)(\theta-1)}. \]

Using

\[ \mu\equiv \frac{1}{\theta-1}, \]

this becomes

\[ \frac{P_t(i)}{P_t} = MC_t\frac{1+\mu}{1-\tau_t}. \]

So the firm sets price as a markup over marginal cost.

This is the second key economic idea of the model:

  • monopolistic competition creates a wedge between price and marginal cost;
  • but if prices are still flexible, money is still neutral;
  • so markup pricing alone is not enough.

4. Why sticky prices are introduced

At this stage the model is more realistic than perfect competition, but monetary policy still has no real effect if all firms can adjust prices immediately.

So the next step is exactly the one needed to break classical dichotomy:

not all firms can reoptimize every period.

Ferrero uses Calvo pricing. This is not because anyone thinks firms literally toss coins. It is because Calvo gives nominal rigidity without forcing us to track the full cross-sectional distribution of price vintages.

Assumption:

  • with probability \(1-\alpha\), a firm resets its price,
  • with probability \(\alpha\), it keeps the old price.

The crucial implication is dynamic pricing:

  • when a firm resets today, it knows the price may remain in place for many future periods,
  • so it chooses a price by trading off expected future demand and expected future marginal costs.

5. Calvo problem and optimal reset price

A firm resetting in period \(t\) chooses \(\widetilde P_t(i)\) to maximize the discounted present value of profits while its price remains fixed:

\[ \max_{\widetilde P_t(i)} E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j} \left[ (1-\tau_{t+j})\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}-MC_{t+j} \right] Y_{t+j|t}(i) \]

subject to

\[ Y_{t+j|t}(i)=Y_{t+j}\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}\right)^{-\theta}, \]

and

\[ M_{t,t+j}=\beta^j\left(\frac{C_{t+j}}{C_t}\right)^{-\sigma}. \]

Substitute demand into the objective:

\[ \max_{\widetilde P_t(i)} E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j} \left[ (1-\tau_{t+j})\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}\right)^{1-\theta} - MC_{t+j}\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}\right)^{-\theta} \right]Y_{t+j}. \]

FOC with respect to \(\widetilde P_t(i)\):

\[ E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j}(1-\tau_{t+j})(1-\theta) \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}\right)^{-\theta} \frac{Y_{t+j}}{P_{t+j}} \]

\[ \qquad + E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j}\theta MC_{t+j} \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_{t+j}}\right)^{-\theta-1} \frac{Y_{t+j}}{P_{t+j}} =0. \]

Rearranging yields the standard reset-price condition

\[ E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j}(1-\tau_{t+j}) \frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_t} \left(\frac{P_{t+j}}{P_t}\right)^{\theta-1} Y_{t+j} \]

\[ = (1+\mu) E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j} \left(\frac{P_{t+j}}{P_t}\right)^{\theta} MC_{t+j}Y_{t+j}. \]

Define cumulative gross inflation between \(t\) and \(t+j\) as

\[ \Pi_{t+j,t}\equiv \frac{P_{t+j}}{P_t}. \]

Then

\[ \frac{\widetilde P_t(i)}{P_t} = (1+\mu) \frac{ E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j}\Pi_{t+j,t}^{\theta}MC_{t+j}Y_{t+j} }{ E_t\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\alpha^j M_{t,t+j}(1-\tau_{t+j})\Pi_{t+j,t}^{\theta-1}Y_{t+j} }. \]

In symmetric equilibrium all reoptimizing firms choose the same reset price, so write

\[ \frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}=\frac{X_{1t}}{X_{2t}}, \]

where

\[ X_{1t} = (1+\mu)MC_tY_t + \alpha E_t\left(M_{t,t+1}\Pi_{t+1}^{\theta}X_{1,t+1}\right), \]

\[ X_{2t} = (1-\tau_t)Y_t + \alpha E_t\left(M_{t,t+1}\Pi_{t+1}^{\theta-1}X_{2,t+1}\right). \]

This notation is not cosmetic. It isolates the two parts of the pricing problem:

  • \(X_{1t}\): discounted expected future costs,
  • \(X_{2t}\): discounted expected future demand/revenues.

So the reset price is a dynamic markup formula:

desired reset price = markup × discounted future marginal cost, normalized by discounted future demand conditions.

6. Aggregate price level: why inflation is a mix of old and new prices

Now we need to connect the reset price to aggregate inflation.

By definition of the CES price index,

\[ P_t^{1-\theta}=\int_0^1 P_t(i)^{1-\theta}\,di. \]

Under Calvo:

  • a fraction \(\alpha\) keeps last period’s price,
  • a fraction \(1-\alpha\) resets to \(\widetilde P_t\).

Hence

\[ P_t^{1-\theta} = \alpha P_{t-1}^{1-\theta} + (1-\alpha)\widetilde P_t^{\,1-\theta}. \]

Dividing by \(P_t^{1-\theta}\) gives

\[ 1=\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}+(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{1-\theta}, \]

where

\[ \Pi_t\equiv \frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}. \]

Using \(\widetilde P_t/P_t=X_{1t}/X_{2t}\),

\[ 1=\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}+(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{X_{1t}}{X_{2t}}\right)^{1-\theta}. \]

This is the nonlinear relation between reset pricing and inflation.

The economic meaning is simple:

  • inflation is high when new reset prices are high relative to old prices,
  • but only the adjusting firms can move the aggregate price level immediately,
  • so inflation is sluggish because many firms are mechanically stuck with yesterday’s price.

That is the source of nominal inertia.

7. Price dispersion: why sticky prices create inefficiency

Sticky prices imply that identical firms charge different prices. Hence they sell different quantities even though they have the same technology.

This creates price dispersion, which is a resource misallocation.

Start from production:

\[ Y_t(i)=A_tN_t(i). \]

Integrating across firms:

\[ \int_0^1 Y_t(i)\,di=A_t\int_0^1 N_t(i)\,di=A_tN_t. \]

Using demand,

\[ \int_0^1 Y_t(i)\,di = \int_0^1 Y_t\left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di = Y_t\Delta_t, \]

where

\[ \Delta_t\equiv \int_0^1 \left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di. \]

So aggregate production becomes

\[ Y_t\Delta_t=A_tN_t. \]

If all firms charged the same price, then \(\Delta_t=1\), and we would recover the efficient aggregate production relation \(Y_t=A_tN_t\). But with sticky prices, typically \(\Delta_t>1\): the same aggregate labor input produces less effective aggregate output.

To derive the law of motion of \(\Delta_t\), split firms into non-adjusters and adjusters:

\[ \Delta_t = \int_0^1 \left(\frac{P_t(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di = \int_0^\alpha \left(\frac{P_{t-1}(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di + \int_\alpha^1 \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di. \]

The first term is

\[ \int_0^\alpha \left(\frac{P_{t-1}(i)}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di = \int_0^\alpha \left(\frac{P_{t-1}(i)}{P_{t-1}}\right)^{-\theta} \left(\frac{P_{t-1}}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di = \alpha \Pi_t^\theta \Delta_{t-1}. \]

The second term is

\[ \int_\alpha^1 \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}di = (1-\alpha)\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

Thus

\[ \Delta_t = \alpha \Pi_t^\theta \Delta_{t-1} + (1-\alpha)\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta}. \]

From the price-index relation

\[ 1=\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}+(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{1-\theta}, \]

we obtain

\[ \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{1-\theta} = \frac{1-\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}}{1-\alpha}, \]

hence

\[ \left(\frac{\widetilde P_t}{P_t}\right)^{-\theta} = \left( \frac{1-\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}}{1-\alpha} \right)^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}}. \]

Therefore the law of motion for price dispersion is

\[ \Delta_t = \alpha \Pi_t^\theta \Delta_{t-1} + (1-\alpha) \left( \frac{1-\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}}{1-\alpha} \right)^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}}. \]

This object matters because it is the extra inefficiency created by sticky prices, over and above monopoly power.

8. Households: why demand enters the model

We now close general equilibrium. Up to now, firms were solving pricing problems, but we still need aggregate demand.

The representative household solves

\[ \max_{\{C_t,B_t,N_t\}} E_0\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\beta^t \left( \frac{C_t^{1-\sigma}}{1-\sigma} - \frac{N_t^{1+\phi}}{1+\phi} \right) \]

subject to the budget constraint

\[ P_tC_t+B_t=(1+i_t)B_{t-1}+W_tN_t+\text{profits}_t. \]

FOCs:

Euler equation

\[ C_t^{-\sigma} = \beta E_t\left[ C_{t+1}^{-\sigma}\frac{1+i_t}{\Pi_{t+1}} \right]. \]

Labor supply

\[ \frac{W_t}{P_t}=N_t^\phi C_t^\sigma. \]

Goods market clearing gives

\[ Y_t=C_t. \]

Now combine labor supply with real marginal cost:

\[ MC_t=\frac{W_t}{A_tP_t}. \]

Thus

\[ A_tMC_t=\frac{W_t}{P_t}=N_t^\phi C_t^\sigma. \]

Using \(C_t=Y_t\) and from \(Y_t\Delta_t=A_tN_t\),

\[ N_t=\frac{Y_t\Delta_t}{A_t}. \]

Hence

\[ A_tMC_t = \left(\frac{Y_t\Delta_t}{A_t}\right)^\phi Y_t^\sigma, \]

so

\[ A_t^{1+\phi}MC_t = Y_t^{\sigma+\phi}\Delta_t^\phi. \]

This is a crucial link:

demand affects output, output affects labor, labor affects wages, wages affect marginal cost, and marginal cost drives inflation.

That is the transmission mechanism of the model in one line.

9. Nonlinear equilibrium system

Given exogenous productivity \(A_t\) and the policy rate \(i_t\), equilibrium is a sequence \(\{Y_t,MC_t,\Pi_t,\Delta_t,X_{1t},X_{2t}\}\) satisfying

\[ Y_t^{-\sigma} = \beta E_t\left[ Y_{t+1}^{-\sigma}\frac{1+i_t}{\Pi_{t+1}} \right], \]

\[ A_t^{1+\phi}MC_t = Y_t^{\sigma+\phi}\Delta_t^\phi, \]

\[ 1=\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}+(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{X_{1t}}{X_{2t}}\right)^{1-\theta}, \]

\[ X_{1t} = (1+\mu)MC_tY_t + \alpha\beta E_t\left[ \left(\frac{Y_{t+1}}{Y_t}\right)^{-\sigma} \Pi_{t+1}^{\theta} X_{1,t+1} \right], \]

\[ X_{2t} = (1-\tau_t)Y_t + \alpha\beta E_t\left[ \left(\frac{Y_{t+1}}{Y_t}\right)^{-\sigma} \Pi_{t+1}^{\theta-1} X_{2,t+1} \right], \]

\[ \Delta_t = \alpha \Pi_t^\theta \Delta_{t-1} + (1-\alpha) \left( \frac{1-\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}}{1-\alpha} \right)^{\frac{\theta}{\theta-1}}. \]

This is the fully nonlinear NK model in this simple environment.

10. Efficient steady state: why we compute it

The model contains two distortions:

  1. monopoly power, through the markup;
  2. sticky-price misallocation, through price dispersion.

To define the output gap, we need a benchmark allocation. Ferrero chooses the efficient steady state: zero inflation, no price dispersion, and subsidy chosen to neutralize the monopoly wedge.

Assume steady state:

\[ \Pi=1, \qquad \Delta=1. \]

Then from the production block:

\[ Y=AN. \]

From the inflation equation,

\[ 1=\alpha +(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{X_1}{X_2}\right)^{1-\theta} \quad \Rightarrow \quad X_1=X_2. \]

In steady state,

\[ X_1=(1+\mu)MC\,Y+\alpha\beta X_1, \]

so

\[ X_1=\frac{(1+\mu)MC\,Y}{1-\alpha\beta}. \]

Similarly,

\[ X_2=(1-\tau)Y+\alpha\beta X_2, \]

so

\[ X_2=\frac{(1-\tau)Y}{1-\alpha\beta}. \]

Since \(X_1=X_2\),

\[ \frac{(1+\mu)MC\,Y}{1-\alpha\beta} = \frac{(1-\tau)Y}{1-\alpha\beta}, \]

hence

\[ (1+\mu)MC=1-\tau. \]

For the efficient steady state we want

\[ MC=1. \]

So set the subsidy to remove the markup distortion:

\[ \tau=-\mu. \]

This is why the tax/subsidy term was introduced earlier: it lets the flexible-price steady state coincide with the undistorted RBC allocation.

Now use labor-market equilibrium. Since \(C=Y\) and \(\Delta=1\),

\[ A\,MC=N^\phi C^\sigma. \]

At the efficient steady state \(MC=1\), so

\[ A=N^\phi Y^\sigma. \]

Since \(Y=AN\), we have \(N=Y/A\). Therefore

\[ A=\left(\frac{Y}{A}\right)^\phi Y^\sigma = Y^{\sigma+\phi}A^{-\phi}. \]

Multiply by \(A^\phi\):

\[ A^{1+\phi}=Y^{\sigma+\phi}. \]

Hence

\[ Y^* = A^{\frac{1+\phi}{\sigma+\phi}}. \]

This is the natural or efficient level of output in this no-capital economy.

So the efficient steady state matters because it gives the benchmark around which the model is approximated, and relative to which the output gap is defined.

11. Log-linearization: why we approximate around the efficient steady state

The nonlinear system cannot generally be solved in closed form. So we log-linearize around the efficient steady state:

  • zero inflation: \(\Pi=1\),
  • no price dispersion: \(\Delta=1\),
  • monopoly distortion removed by subsidy.

To first order, price dispersion drops out:

\[ \widehat\Delta_t=0. \]

This is why the three-equation NK model looks so much cleaner than the nonlinear system. The ugly cross-sectional object is second order.

12. New Keynesian Phillips Curve

Start from

\[ 1=\alpha \Pi_t^{\theta-1}+(1-\alpha)\left(\frac{X_{1t}}{X_{2t}}\right)^{1-\theta}. \]

Log-linearizing around zero inflation steady state gives, after substituting the linearized \(X_{1t}\) and \(X_{2t}\) recursions,

\[ \pi_t = \frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\alpha\beta)}{\alpha} \left(mc_t+\widehat\tau_t\right) + \beta E_t\pi_{t+1}. \]

This is the baseline NK Phillips curve.

Economic interpretation:

  • inflation rises when current real marginal cost rises,
  • inflation also rises when firms expect future marginal costs to be high,
  • and cost-push disturbances can shift inflation even for a given output gap.

Now relate marginal cost to output.

From the log-linearized labor-cost relation around the efficient steady state,

\[ (1+\phi)a_t+mc_t=(\sigma+\phi)y_t. \]

At the efficient allocation, \(mc_t^*=0\), so

\[ y_t^*=\frac{1+\phi}{\sigma+\phi}a_t. \]

Therefore

\[ mc_t=(\sigma+\phi)(y_t-y_t^*). \]

Define the output gap

\[ x_t\equiv y_t-y_t^*. \]

Then

\[ mc_t=(\sigma+\phi)x_t. \]

Substitute into the Phillips curve:

\[ \pi_t = \frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\alpha\beta)(\sigma+\phi)}{\alpha}x_t + \beta E_t\pi_{t+1} + \frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\alpha\beta)}{\alpha}\widehat\tau_t. \]

Define

\[ \kappa \equiv \frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\alpha\beta)(\sigma+\phi)}{\alpha}, \]

and let

\[ u_t \equiv \frac{(1-\alpha)(1-\alpha\beta)}{\alpha}\widehat\tau_t. \]

Then

\[ \pi_t=\kappa x_t+\beta E_t\pi_{t+1}+u_t. \]

That is the New Keynesian Phillips Curve.

The logic is decisive:

  • sticky prices make inflation depend on desired price adjustment,
  • desired price adjustment depends on marginal cost,
  • marginal cost depends on the output gap.

So inflation is ultimately pinned down by real activity and expectations.

13. Dynamic IS curve

Now log-linearize the Euler equation

\[ Y_t^{-\sigma} = \beta E_t\left[ Y_{t+1}^{-\sigma}\frac{1+i_t}{\Pi_{t+1}} \right]. \]

To first order this gives

\[ y_t = E_ty_{t+1} - \sigma^{-1}(i_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}) + \text{terms involving the natural level}. \]

Rewrite in output-gap form. Since

\[ x_t=y_t-y_t^*, \]

we obtain

\[ x_t = -\sigma^{-1}(i_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}-r_t^*) + E_tx_{t+1}, \]

where the efficient real interest rate is

\[ r_t^* \equiv \sigma(E_ty_{t+1}^*-y_t^*). \]

This is the dynamic IS curve.

Interpretation:

  • when the real interest rate \(i_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}\) rises above the natural rate \(r_t^*\), households want to postpone consumption,
  • demand falls,
  • output falls below efficient output,
  • so the output gap decreases.

This is the demand side of the model.

14. Monetary policy rule

The model is closed with an interest-rate rule:

\[ i_t=\phi_\pi \pi_t+\phi_x x_t+\varepsilon_t. \]

Interpretation:

  • the central bank raises the nominal rate when inflation is high,
  • it may also react to the output gap,
  • \(\varepsilon_t\) is a monetary policy shock.

This is why Ferrero can discuss monetary policy without modeling money explicitly: the policy instrument is the short nominal interest rate.

15. The three-equation New Keynesian model

Putting the pieces together:

Aggregate supply

\[ \pi_t=\kappa x_t+\beta E_t\pi_{t+1}+u_t. \]

Aggregate demand

\[ x_t = -\sigma^{-1}(i_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}-r_t^*) + E_tx_{t+1}. \]

Monetary policy

\[ i_t=\phi_\pi \pi_t+\phi_x x_t+\varepsilon_t. \]

This is the famous three-equation system.

But one should not think of it as three arbitrary equations dropped from heaven. Each equation comes from a specific block:

  • Phillips curve: optimal price setting under Calvo frictions;
  • IS curve: household Euler equation;
  • Taylor rule: central bank behavior.

16. Shocks and interpretation

A convenient representation is:

Supply side

\[ \pi_t=\kappa x_t+\beta E_t\pi_{t+1}+u_t, \qquad u_t=\rho_u u_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t^u. \]

Demand side

\[ x_t = -\sigma^{-1}(i_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}-r_t^*) + E_tx_{t+1}, \]

with

\[ r_t^* = \sigma(E_ty_{t+1}^*-y_t^*). \]

If productivity follows

\[ a_t=\rho_a a_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t^a, \]

then since

\[ y_t^*=\frac{1+\phi}{\sigma+\phi}a_t, \]

it follows that

\[ r_t^* = \sigma\frac{1+\phi}{\sigma+\phi}(E_ta_{t+1}-a_t). \]

Monetary policy

\[ i_t=\phi_\pi\pi_t+\phi_xx_t+\varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t=\rho_\varepsilon \varepsilon_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t^m. \]

So the model typically features:

  • cost-push shocks \(u_t\),
  • natural-rate / productivity shocks through \(r_t^*\),
  • monetary policy shocks \(\varepsilon_t\).

17. Equilibrium determinacy and the Taylor principle

Substitute the Taylor rule into the IS curve:

\[ x_t = -\sigma^{-1} \left( \phi_\pi\pi_t+\phi_xx_t+\varepsilon_t-E_t\pi_{t+1}-r_t^* \right) + E_tx_{t+1}. \]

Together with

\[ \pi_t=\kappa x_t+\beta E_t\pi_{t+1}+u_t, \]

this gives a forward-looking \(2\times 2\) system in \((x_t,\pi_t)\).

In matrix form one can write

\[ A_0 \begin{pmatrix} E_tx_{t+1}\\ E_t\pi_{t+1} \end{pmatrix} = A_1 \begin{pmatrix} x_t\\ \pi_t \end{pmatrix} + Bv_t, \]

with

\[ A_0= \begin{pmatrix} \sigma & 1\\ 0 & \beta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A_1= \begin{pmatrix} \sigma+\phi_x & \phi_\pi\\ \kappa & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ E_tz_{t+1}=A z_t + \widetilde B v_t, \qquad A=A_0^{-1}A_1. \]

Determinacy requires the Blanchard-Kahn condition: the number of unstable eigenvalues must equal the number of forward-looking variables.

In this two-equation purely forward-looking setup, this becomes the requirement that both eigenvalues of \(A\) lie outside the unit circle. This yields the familiar restriction

\[ \phi_\pi > 1-\frac{(1-\beta)\phi_x}{\kappa}. \]

In the special case \(\phi_x=0\), this reduces to

\[ \phi_\pi>1. \]

That is the Taylor principle.

Its economic meaning is not mysterious:

when inflation rises, the central bank must raise the nominal rate by more than one-for-one, so that the real rate rises.

If not, inflationary expectations can become self-fulfilling. Human beings do love building systems that explode unless someone promises to overreact in exactly the right way.

18. Final logic of the whole derivation

The derivation is best read as a chain:

  1. Retailers are introduced to generate a clean CES demand system for differentiated goods.
  2. Wholesalers have monopoly power, so they choose prices as markups over marginal cost.
  3. With flexible prices, this is still basically an RBC world with a static monopoly distortion.
  4. So we introduce Calvo pricing, which makes price-setting forward-looking and gives inflation inertia.
  5. Sticky prices create price dispersion, hence inefficient allocation across firms.
  6. Households provide the Euler equation and labor supply, linking demand to marginal cost.
  7. The efficient steady state is computed to remove the monopoly distortion and define a clean benchmark.
  8. Log-linearization around that benchmark yields:
    • the NK Phillips curve from pricing,
    • the dynamic IS curve from intertemporal choice,
    • and the Taylor rule from policy.
  9. The model then explains how monetary policy affects output and inflation in the short run, while preserving long-run monetary neutrality.

So the conceptual core is:

a forward-looking demand equation + a forward-looking pricing equation + an interest-rate rule.

Everything else is scaffolding to derive those objects cleanly.

19. Minimal summary

The New Keynesian model is an RBC-style economy without capital, but with two crucial departures:

  • firms have market power,
  • and prices are sticky.

Because of market power, firms care about markups.
Because of sticky prices, they cannot always adjust.
Therefore inflation becomes a forward-looking object tied to expected marginal costs.
And because households smooth consumption intertemporally, the real interest rate affects demand and hence the output gap.

That is why monetary policy has real effects in the short run.
